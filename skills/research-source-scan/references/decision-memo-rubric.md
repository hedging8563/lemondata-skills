# Decision Memo Rubric

## Default stance

- Choose one default stance first: adopt now, trial first, or defer.
- Use the decision rule section to explain when each branch applies, but do not hide behind balanced prose.
- If evidence is mixed, prefer a trial-first stance over a vague maybe.

## Decision usefulness

- A good memo reduces decision friction for the reader, not just summarizes research.
- Each evidence bullet should answer: what changes if this fact is true?
- The bottom line should sound like a recommendation a lead could forward internally.
- Three strong points beat a long list; use exactly three sources, three evidence bullets, and three next actions in the default brief.

## Next action standard

- Make the three next actions testable: define a scope, a short timebox, or a success check.
- Prefer actions like run a 2-day staging pilot, measure added latency, or verify fallback success over generic discovery work.
- Avoid generic verbs such as explore, assess, or consider unless the action also says what to examine.
- Use an explicit `Success check:` sentence for each action so the reader can tell whether the pilot passed or failed.

## Threshold design

- When the decision depends on scale, use concrete thresholds such as provider count, monthly spend band, downtime tolerance, or acceptable latency budget.
- Label internal thresholds as go or no-go bars unless they are directly attributable to a source.
- Quantified decision bars are strongest when they turn vague advice into a forwardable operating recommendation.
